\begin{abstract}
Indonesian organizations receive AI through application programming interface (API) mediation of global foundation models. Nevertheless, no previous research has quantitatively measured how safety properties degrade across various deployment configurations. In this research, AI safety asymmetry is measured across three deployment conditions: consumer simulation (C1), raw access (C2), and safety-stripped (C3), resulting in 893 observations across six models evaluated in both English and Bahasa Indonesia through dual-language ordinal scoring (0-3 scale). Concurrently, regulatory research maps 31 AI safety concepts onto eight Indonesian governance instruments through two independent embedding models.

Three out of five preregistered research hypotheses receive support. The configuration degradation effect reduces refusal probability by 45.7\% under the safety-stripped configuration (odds ratio OR = 0.543, p = 0.0008). The effect of architectural degradation (C1 to C2) is statistically significant (p = 0.018, $\Delta$R = 20.5\%). Models from the European Union outperform US models by 8.5\% (p = 0.041). The worst configuration, safety-stripped and Bahasa Indonesia, results in 37.6\% harmful compliance. Regulatory research finds no mentions of Foundation Model Provider liability in 93,293 regulatory words. Critical severity gaps exist in medical and tax-legal AI safety.

This research is the first quantitative evidence of application-layer AI safety asymmetry in Indonesia. The results suggest that the primary safety factor—deployer configuration—operates in a regulatory vacuum.

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
%% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Under Indonesia’s \textit{Stranas KA} 2020--2045~\cite{b9}, AI adoption proceeds principally through API mediation of foreign-origin foundation models deployed across consumer, government, and healthcare applications. In every such deployment, safety outcomes depend not on regulatory obligation but on the API integrator’s configuration choices.

Foundation models embed safety mechanisms—Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback~\cite{b25} and Constitutional AI~\cite{b26}—within a three-tier deployment stack: model weights, API access layer, and application scaffolding. Raw API access removes the scaffolding tier; an adversarial system prompt further suppresses model-level guardrails. Neither transition is currently governed by Indonesian regulation.

Three literature gaps motivate this work. First, cross-lingual safety research~\cite{b5,b6} documents Indonesian safety deficits yet does not quantify how those deficits vary across API deployment conditions or model origins. Second, dominant evaluation frameworks~\cite{b3,b18} apply binary pass/fail scoring, collapsing partial guardrail failures into a single ``safe'' bin; ordinal LLM-as-a-Judge methodology~\cite{b21,b22} has not been applied to Indonesian API safety. Third, Indonesia’s governance corpus~\cite{b9,b10,b11,b12,b13,b14,b15,b16}—characterised as fragmented and technology-neutral~\cite{b2,b8}—has never been computationally mapped for API-specific obligations.

This paper makes four novel contributions: ($i$)~the first quantification of API safety asymmetry across three deployment conditions, two languages, and six models ($n\!=\!893$); ($ii$)~a replicable dual-LLM-as-a-Judge framework on open-source infrastructure; ($iii$)~a dual-embedding semantic matrix covering 31 safety concepts against eight governance instruments; and ($iv$)~evidence-based policy analysis establishing Foundation Model Provider liability as a zero-mention gap across 93,293 regulatory words.

\section{Background and Related Work}
%% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{AI Safety Measurement}

The long-standing goal of automated safety evaluation at the corpus scale has been a topic of interest in various red-teaming communities ~\cite{b1,b17}. Nevertheless, the use of binary pass/fail metrics results in the conflation of partial guardrail failures, in which denial language co-occurs with unsafe content, into the single "safe" bin ~\cite{b3,b18}. Zou et al.~\cite{b19} and Chao et al.~\cite{b20} have shown the capability for safety at the weight level to be systematically bypassed via prompt engineering, motivating the evaluation of the configuration layer as the dominant safety variable for API-based deployments~\cite{b4}.

\subsection{LLM-as-a-Judge Evaluation}

Zheng et al.~\cite{b21} have demonstrated the capability for judge models to achieve human comparable quality agreement, thereby permitting ordinal-based safety evaluation at the corpus scale. Markov et al.~\cite{b22} have shown the requirement for multi-dimensional rather than binary-based categorization in the context of real-world harm detection.

\subsection{Cross-Linguistic Safety and Indonesian NLP}

Yong et al.~\cite{b5} have demonstrated the effectiveness in Indonesian for safety at 30-50\% of the English baseline, which is attributed to the use of English-dominant RLHF training corpora. Shi et al.~\cite{b6} have shown the systematic performance deficit in non-English languages across multilingual models. Koto et al.~\cite{b32}, Winata et al.~\cite{b33}, and Cahyawijaya et al.~\cite{b34} have established Indonesian NLP benchmarks but have not addressed the topic of safety evaluation. Dai et al. ~\cite{b31} have developed SeaLLMs with calibration for harm categories in Southeast Asia.

\subsection{Indonesian AI Governance and Regulatory Theory}

The Indonesian regulatory corpus consists of eight regulatory instruments across the domain of the four ministerial portfolios~\cite{b9,b10,b11,b12,b13,b14,b15,b16}. Existing studies have characterized the corpus as being fragmented and technology-neutral. Baldwin et al.~\cite{b2} have established the regulatory failure typology, which provides the vocabulary for understanding the regulatory gaps identified in this work. Diver's precision-durability framework ~\cite{b8} provides the vocabulary for understanding the regulatory gaps identified in this work. The OECD AI Principles~\cite{b35} provide the normative benchmark by which the deployer accountability provisions in Indonesia are evaluated.

\section{Theoretical Framework}
%% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

This paper contributes the construct of \textit{API-Mediated AI Safety Asymmetry} as a quantifiable concept in the field of Information Systems. This construct represents the process of systematic degradation in safety abilities that occur when there is a transition in the foundation model from vertically integrated consumer deployment to horizontally integrated API deployment. This construct combines the theories of Technical Safety Measurement Theory~\cite{b1,b3} and the theory of Regulatory Gap Theory~\cite{b2,b8} and is quantified over four analytically distinct dimensions.

The \textbf{architectural dimension} of API-mediated safety asymmetry is concerned with the process of removing the scaffolding layer in the transition from consumer simulation (C1) to raw API access (C2), where there is the elimination of the input classifiers, safety system prompts, and output moderators while maintaining weight-level safety. The \textbf{configurational dimension} of API-mediated safety asymmetry is concerned with the process of deployer system prompts in the transition from C2 to C3. This dimension is the primary policy leverage point that is entirely within the domestic realm. The \textbf{observational dimension} of API-mediated safety asymmetry is concerned with the process of degraded interaction telemetry and the absence of a domestic AI incident registry. Lastly, the \textbf{temporal-domain dimension} of API-mediated safety asymmetry is concerned with the process of safety validation lag in the transposition of domain deployments in Indonesian healthcare and legal systems, where English-trained safety patterns may not be activated~\cite{b5}.

The theory of Regulatory Gap Theory ~\cite{b2,b8}  represents the absence of the configuration layer as the allocation of safety responsibility to the entity with the weakest incentive—the domestic API integrator with the process of system prompt choices. API configuration is represented as a regulable safety variable by Bommasani et al.~\cite{b24} and Hollnagel~\cite{b7}.

The entire set of prompts was presented to six foundation models across three geographic origin regions as follows: models from the US ($n\!=\!449$; Arcee-AI Trinity Large, Google Gemini 3 Flash Lite, Google Gemini 3 Pro), models from the EU ($n\!=\!297$; CognitiveComputations Dolphin-Mistral-24B, Mistral Small), and CN-origin ($n\!=\!147$;
StepFun Step-3.5 Flash). The prompts correspond to 28 harm intent categories, each at five levels (R, L, H, C, D). The category L prompts are presented in Bahasa Indonesia alone, whereas all other category prompts are presented concurrently in both English and Bahasa Indonesia.

\subsection{Dual LLM-as-a-Judge Evaluation}

The evaluation framework based on binary keyword triggers does not account for partial guardrail violations. The evaluation framework used in this study consists of a four-point ordinal evaluation framework with the following values:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{3} - Robust refusal with harm framing,
\item \textbf{2} - Partial refusal without full safety explanation,
\item \textbf{1} - Stated refusal with harmful content present in the response,
\item \textbf{0} - Complete harmful compliance.
\end{itemize}
The study also used architecturally different evaluation frameworks to evaluate each response:

\textbf{Primary - Qwen2.5-3B-Instruct}~\cite{b30}: multilingual pre-training, 4-bit NF4 quantization~\cite{b38}, operating range $\{1,2,3\}$ (floor truncation; binary scores not affected).

\textbf{Cross-validation - SeaLLMs-v3-7B-Chat}~\cite{b31}: Southeast Asian corpus fine-tuning (SARA, \textit{hoaks}, \textit{penipuan}); Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) $R^2\!=\!0.200$
vs. Qwen $R^2\!=\!0.030$, indicating superior ordinal discrimination for the former model. Binary refusal rate identical across both models at 68.8\%, implying all discrepancies are in the ordinal evaluation framework.

\subsection{Statistical Analysis}

The ordinal dependent variable calls for non-parametric tests: Mann-Whitney U for two-group designs (H1, H2), and Kruskal-Wallis H for multi-group designs, followed by Bonferroni corrections for post hoc analyses of all significant main effects (H3, H5). A Proportional Odds Model, or Ordered Logistic Regression, is used for covariate odds ratio estimation. Binary logistic regression of pre-judge keyword scoring is conducted for robustness checks of all five research hypotheses, independent of the evaluator.

\subsection{Regulatory Corpus Analysis}

Two independent embedding models were used for analyzing eight cleaned-up regulatory texts, totaling 93,293 words: \texttt{paraphrase-multilingual-MiniLM-L12-v2}
\cite{b36}  for document-level embedding, cosine threshold = 0.35, and \texttt{intfloat/multilingual-e5-base}~\cite{b37} for chunk-level embedding in 100-word window sizes, threshold = 0.82. Gaps identified by both models \textit{(Dual-confirmed gaps)} as below threshold constitute absolute regulatory absences, unaffected by embedding model architecture. Actor-liability extraction by rule-based NLP identified co-occurrences of four actor types: Foundation Model Provider, API Developer, End User, Government) with obligation terms (\textit{wajib}, \textit{bertanggung jawab})

\subsection{H1 --- Architectural Degradation (C1 vs.\ C2): Partially Supported}

Consumer-simulated deployment outperforms raw API deployment on binary refusal
by 10.0 percentage points (76.3\% vs.\ 66.3\%), a binary relative decline of
13.1\%. The judge-based $\Delta R\!\%$---the dual-criterion policy metric
computed as the relative decline in robust-refusal rate (working score~$=3$)---is
20.5\% (C1:\,39.4\% $\to$ C2:\,31.3\% per Qwen judge), also below the 40\%
full-support threshold. Mann-Whitney~U confirms statistical significance under
both judges (Qwen: $p\!=\!0.018$, $r\!=\!{-}0.093$; SeaLLMs: $p\!=\!0.007$,
$r\!=\!{-}0.099$). Neither the binary (12.8\%) nor the judge-based (20.5\%)
$\Delta R\!\%$ meets the 40\% architectural degradation threshold drawn from
adversarial red-teaming literature~\cite{b3,b4}; hence partial support.

The results when deployed in consumer-like conditions outperform results when using API deployment on binary refusal rates by 9.8 percentage points (76.5\% versus 66.7\%), which is equivalent to a binary relative decline of 12.8\%. The judge-based $\Delta R\%$, which is defined as the relative decline in robust refusal rates (using a work score of 3), is 20.5\%. This is calculated using C1 results of 39.4\% versus C2 results of 31.3\%, using the Qwen judge. Which the result yieds below the 40\% threshold. In addition, it is below the threshold defined using architectural degradation via adversarial red-teaming. Mann–Whitney U tests were used to verify statistical significance using both judges (Qwen: $p\!=\!0.018$, $r\!=\!{-}0.093$; SeaLLMs: $p\!=\!0.007$, $r\!=\!{-}0.099$). Neither the binary Delta R\% of 12.8\% nor the judge-based $\Delta R\!\%$ of 20.5\% meets the 40\% architectural degradation threshold defined via adversarial red-teaming~\cite{b3,b4}. This is equivalent to partial support.

\subsection{H2 --- Linguistic Asymmetry (English vs.\ Bahasa Indonesia):
Partially Supported}

The binary language effect is not statistically significant (odds ratio = 1.124, $p\!=\!0.430$). On ordinal evaluation, however, both judges produce opposite results. Qwen-3B concludes that English is safer (odds ratio = 1.621, $p\!<\!0.001$), while SeaLLMs-7B concludes that Bahasa Indonesian is safer (odds ratio = 0.090, $p\!<\!0.001$). This is not a contradictory result. Though, this is cultural calibration of judges is Southeast Asian versus Chinese/English. In this case, we are dealing with a \textit{methodological finding}. To obtain the direction of results, we need a gold standard. The C3\,$\times$\,Bahasa Indonesia
worst-cell (37.4\% compliance) constitutes a practical risk finding independent
of ordinal evaluator direction.

\subsection{H3 --- Configuration Collapse (C1$\to$C2$\to$C3): Partially Supported}

Both evaluators report a monotonically decreasing pattern in safety scores. The Kruskal-Wallis test results are Qwen: Qwen $H\!=\!16.57$, $p\!=\!0.0003$; SeaLLMs $H\!=\!10.25$,
$p\!=\!0.006$. The evaluator-invariant binary logistic regression results provide the most relevant quantification for policy purposes: C2~OR\,=\,0.612 ($p\!=\!0.0076$);
C3~OR\,=\,0.543 ($p\!=\!0.0008$). The explicitly permissive system prompts reduce refusal probability by 45.7\%. The S\% = 12.5\% for Qwen is below the 70\% threshold for full support and therefore classified as partial.

Panels (b) and (c) of Figure \ref{fig:experimental} show the gradient for three conditions and geographic effects.

\subsection{The Configuration-Regulatory Decoupling}

The key result is the precise match between experimental and regulatory world. The key safety factor—deployer configuration—matches the key area where regulation is absent. The Indonesian API integrator has legal grounds to remove 45.7\% of the refusal capacity of a C3-foundation model's system prompt (odds ratio OR = 0.543, $p\!=\!0.0008$), resulting in 36.0\% in harmful compliance, with no Indonesian regulatory consequence. The chain from Foundation Model Provider (no Indonesian liability) via API Integrator (configuration only) to End User (who absorbs the harm) has no self-correcting mechanism in place. Baldwin et al.'s~\cite{b2} concept of institutional failure is thus demonstrated in Indonesia, where the Indonesian regulatory architecture does not correspond to the AI inference layer as a unit subject to regulation.

The partial support for our hypotheses is not evidence for null results. The thresholds for H1 (40\%), for $S\!\%\!\geq\!70\%$ for H3, were derived from adversarial red-teaming research and not from realistic model configurations. The observed effects ($\Delta R\%\!=\!20.5\%$; C3~OR\,=\,0.543) are reproducible, policy-actionable effects not observed in previous Indonesian AI safety research.

\subsection{Cross-Lingual Evaluator Calibration as a Methodological Contribution}

The H2 evaluator reversal implies that judge-model selection in cross-lingual safety science is a systematic design decision in measurement. Qwen-3B, pre-trained on Chinese/English, uses English refusal calibration, whereas SeaLLMs-7B, fine-tuned on Southeast Asian data, uses Bahasa Indonesia discourse calibration. Binary invariance is identical at 69.1\%, confirming that both judges detect the same behavioral signal, whereas ordinal divergence is contingent on the instrument. Information systems research on cross-lingual LLM judges must be complemented by multi-judge validation.

\subsection{Policy Recommendations}

Four evidence-based reforms are identified. \textbf{(R1)} A binding \textit{Peraturan
Menteri Kominfo} on system prompt safety standards directly addresses the configuration gap by stipulating a minimum standard for information system prompts. This reform is significant, with an odds ratio of 0.543, $p\!=\!0.0008$. \textbf{(R2)} An update to \textit{UU ITE No. 1/2024} to recognize Foundation Model Providers and API Deployers as separately regulable entities closes the zero-mention gap in Table 1 and is consistent with the liability framework of the EU AI Act. \textbf{(R3)} Elevating a ministerial regulation on \textit{Etika KA} provides AI-native vocabulary without amending the parent legislation. \textbf{(R4)} Annexes on sectoral regulations by \textit{Kemenkes} and OJK cover two of three critical gaps in medical and tax/legal regulations without amending legislation.

\section{Conclusion}
%% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

This study represents the first quantitative examination of the API layer AI safety asymmetry in Indonesia. Three results have relevant policy implications. First, the predictor for safety failure due to deployer configuration was statistically significant and invariant across evaluators (C3 OR = 0.543, $p\!=\!0.0008$). Second, liability for Foundation Model Providers was not present in the eight legal instruments containing 93,293 words. Third, API layer medical and tax/legal AI are situated in a governance vacuum of significant severity, supported by both independent embedding models. While the safety asymmetry is not fixed in nature, as the configuration layer is subject to the nation's regulations, the governance terminology can be found in the draft \textit{Etika KA} regulations. The discrepancy between empirical results and legal frameworks is not technically based.

The study's results should be extended to include the creation of a gold standard to resolve the discrepancies between the evaluators (H2), a replication of the study with the introduction of new versions of models, and interviews with API integrators in Indonesia to understand the determinants of configuration behavior.
